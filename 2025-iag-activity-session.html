Introductions

For this session, we will first look at accessibility responsibilities for two groups of designers:

  • Product Designers
  • Research Designers

We will then go to a FigJam breakout session where we explore these responsibilities in detail.

Accessibility Checklist for Product Designers

Adaptable UI
  • Overall layouts can scale without loss of content or functionality
  • Text within layouts can scale without breaking layout or hiding content
  • Text and letter spacing can be adjusted without disrupting the layout
  • Dark mode or high contrast themes are available
  • Layouts support responsive design across different devices and orientations
Colour Contrast
  • Text has sufficient colour contrast against its background
  • UI components (e.g. buttons, icons, form fields) have sufficient contrast in all states
  • Colour is not used as the only means of conveying meaning
Headings
  • Heading levels follow a logical hierarchy without skipping levels (e.g. h1 → h2 → h3)
  • Heading content is meaningful and descriptive
  • Each page has a single H1 element as the primary heading
Images
  • Image types are clearly identified: decorative, informative, functional, or complex
  • Informative and functional images have appropriate text alternatives
  • Decorative images are marked appropriately
  • Complex images include long descriptions or data table alternatives
Links and Buttons
  • Links and buttons are used correctly (e.g. links navigate; buttons trigger actions)
  • Links and buttons have clear, consistent, and meaningful labels
  • Links and buttons provide appropriate visual and keyboard focus states
Forms
  • Labels, instructions, and error messages are positioned close to the relevant fields
  • All form fields have visible and programmatically associated labels
  • Instructions are clear and concise
  • Required fields are clearly indicated
  • Errors are easy to find, understand, and correct
  • Colour is not the only way that errors or required fields are identified
Focus Management
  • Focus moves in a logical, expected order when using the keyboard
  • Focus is managed appropriately for dynamic content (e.g. modals, form submissions)
  • Focus is never lost or trapped within components
Multimedia
  • Captions are provided for all video content
  • Transcripts are provided for all audio and video content
  • Audio descriptions are available for important visual-only content
  • Autoplaying media is avoided or can be paused/stopped by the user

Accessibility and Inclusion Checklist for Research Designers

Planning Inclusive Research
  • Research goals consider the needs of people with disabilities and diverse lived experiences
  • Research methods support participation from people with a range of abilities
  • Consent processes are accessible and understandable
  • Research venues (virtual or physical) are accessible to all participants
  • Timing, pacing, and breaks are flexible to accommodate participant needs
Recruitment and Representation
  • Recruitment includes people with disabilities and other underrepresented groups
  • Screening criteria do not unintentionally exclude people with disabilities
  • Participants are not tokenised or treated as edge cases
  • Research includes a range of access needs and disabilities
Communication and Facilitation
  • Communication before, during, and after sessions is clear, respectful, and accessible
  • Participants can choose their preferred communication method
  • Accessibility needs are confirmed in advance, with accommodations provided without burden
  • Sessions are facilitated with sensitivity to fatigue, processing time, and emotional impact
  • Participants can share feedback in a format that works for them
Analysis and Synthesis
  • Insights from disabled participants are given equal weight in analysis
  • Patterns in access barriers or inclusive design opportunities are explicitly considered
  • Assumptions and stereotypes are avoided when interpreting participant behaviour or feedback
  • Where possible/relevant, work with co-designers or advisors with lived experience to validate findings
Ethics and Respect
  • Participants are fairly compensated for their time and expertise
  • Language used in documentation respects people’s identities and preferences
  • The emotional impact of asking participants to relive access barriers or exclusion is considered — provide support options if appropriate
  • Power dynamics are acknowledged — participants are treated as partners, not subjects
  • Research outcomes are shared with participants where possible, in accessible formats

Let’s go to the FigJam board!

For each Product Design responsibility, I will ask you to explore the following:

  1. Who has primary responsibility for this accessibility feature?
  2. In which phase would this work best be undertaken?
  3. Where should best practices information about this accessibility feature be documented?
  4. Who is responsible for testing this accessibility feature?
  5. In which phase(s) should this accessibility feature be tested?

For each Research responsibility, I will ask you to explore the following:

  1. How does this checkpoint apply to my current research practice?
  2. What would need to change in our current process to make this a standard practice?
  3. Who else in the team or organisation should be involved in making this happen?
  4. Where should this checkpoint be captured to support future projects?
  5. What barriers might prevent this from happening—and how could they be addressed?
