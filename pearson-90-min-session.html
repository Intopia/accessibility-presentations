People-first language

A person is not defined by their disability – they are a person first.

People-first language declares that a person is not defined by their disability – they are a person first.

People-first language also recognises that disability is a complex phenomenon caused by the interaction of a person with their environment – it doesn't occur in a vacuum.

Disability is not a dirty word. [Euphemisms] actually diminish and erase disability from the picture.

Anjali Forber-Pratt, Vanderbilt University

People with disability want you to say the word disability. Recognising the existence of people who are disabled means recognising the barriers society puts in front of them.

https://news.vanderbilt.edu/2019/04/23/disability-is-not-a-dirty-word-handi-capable-should-be-retired/

Plain language

Plain language is easier to scan.

Plain language is easier for people to scan and understand quickly. It is less wordy. This makes it less daunting for anyone reading online who potentially faces a wall of words.

Plain language is more accessible for a wide range of people.

Plain language is more accessible.

See next page.

  1. People varied learning or cognition.
  2. People with reduced vision.
  3. People using a screen reader.
  4. People reading in a second language.
  5. People who are tired, stressed, short on time, or distracted.

Reduced vision - less content to scan.

Screen reader - easier to listen to content faster.

This includes people whose first language is not English.

It is also easier to translate into another language, making it better for international content or translating into sign languages.

There are 5 principles to writing plain language.

1. Keep words, sentences, and paragraphs short.

A rough rule of thumb is one idea per sentence or paragraph.

Short and familiar words are better than long, formal ones.

Aim for about 15-20 words per sentence. Remember, occasionally you might need a longer sentence so it reads or explains things better.

The ships hung in the sky in much the same way that bricks don't.

Douglas Adams, Hitchhikers Guide to the Galaxy

Plain language is not boring. Single syllabic sentence.

2. Use active voice in the first person.

Active rather than passive voice makes your writing more engaging and friendly becuase active voice is more direct and uses less words.

"Bring a piece of identification and a person to support you."

vs

"Bring your identification and a support person."

For example, ‘Bring a piece of identification and a person who can support you’, is harder to undertand than 'Bring your identification and a support person'.

Writing in the first person means writing as if you were talking directly to your audience, with the authority of someone who can help and inform.

This means using first person terms like I, we, you. Not the customer or users.

3. Use language and terms your reader understands.

Know who your audience is. Use the language and the terms that they use.

Unless you are sure it’s understood by everyone, avoid jargon. Using technical terms, jargon or buzzwords excludes people.

Learn about your reader. Don’t assume you know what words they do or don’t understand.

If you don’t know what words to choose, or you're stuck between several options, use Google trends or Keyword planner to find out. You can also look at your own search engine results to see what words people use to search your site.

"Only a plonker would call time on sozzled bonking."

4. Use left alignment for easier scanning.

Use headings and lists. We’ll talk about this when we discuss grouping information.

The example, shown here, with all text including headings centred, takes more cognitive effort to read.

5. Use headings, tables, and lists to group information.

Use headings and lists. We’ll talk about this now.

Structure

Proximity and grouping helps scanning and association.

Grouping your content helps people scan faster and therefore get to their task sooner.

There are many ways to group content including using headings and paragraphs, lists, tables, and whitespace. We’ll look at these in more detail later.

Close proximity for things like labels and values, or form fields and help text is another valuable tool.

Placing related elements close together and using whitespace to create meaningful groups is a foundational principle in visual design.

Here we have an example of some help text placement makes it confusing about which do or don’t example the help text is referring to.

If the highlighted reasoning was moved closer to the first group of examples, it would be clearer which example it refers to.

Also think about screen magnifier users, or people who zoom in their browsers in order to read. Positioning labels, values or help text on the right of the page increases the chance of being missed. It’s better to have them left aligned, or beneath the thing that they relate to in responsive designs.

  • Should make sense out of context.
  • Should show a page's structure.

Lists help content scanning.

Lists create groups that stand out from the rest of the content and therefore make that content more easily scanned.

  • Use numbered lists when the order of items matters
  • Use bulleted lists where there is no clear order

Lists in sentences add cognitive load. For example:

“In the winter I like to eat a variety of fruit like apples, grapefruit, plums, persimmons, mandarins, papaya, cherries, nectarines, strawberries, apricots, and raspberries to remind me that summer is coming soon.

Many tables should be lists.

This is something I'll just mention quickly, because We’ll talk more about tables later.

Tables are often used when a list is the better option.

If the data isn't relational data, and usually if the data isn't quantitative data, it might make a better list.

Use tables for data rather than layout (except for emails).

Layout tables were common in the 1990s before the invention of CSS, to prevent strange layouts.

It is possible to make them accessible, but your developer will need to do some coding to make it work for everyone.

[Exception is email layout because CSS doesn’t always apply]

Make complex tables simple

While you can code complex tables that have multiple column and row headings, it's easier to convert them into simpler tables. Break up complex tables into simpler tables if you can.

Checking your writing

There are many tools that can help you check your writing, grammar and punctuation and point out areas for improvement. There are other tools that can measure reading level.

As with all tools, these are fine used to help you learn or if you need some extra assurance that you’re on the right track.

However, none of these tools can tell you for certain whether your entire audience will be able to understand your content.

Check that you’re on target with tools.

Online tools like Hemingway app, or Grammarly, or the built-in checker in Microsoft Word can give you tips on how to make your writing clearer and improve grammar and punctuation use.

You can get quite a lot of useful information from free tools, but if you need more, you have to pay for it.

Use readability formulas as a rough gauge.

You can use a readability formula to gauge the reading level of your content. Let’s do an activity now.

Link text

Links must tell you where you’re going.

The most fundamental thing about links is that the text tells you where you’ll go if you follow it.

Think about click bait links that sound intriguing, but when you click them, you don’t end up where you expected. This is not only annoying to your readers but erodes their trust in your site.

I also regularly see links that, rather than take you directly to the content in question, will take you to the site’s home page, in case the site’s structure changes in future.

It’s better to have the links go directly, where possible, straight to the relevant information and deal with possible broken links when you need to.

Link text must be independent.

Link text must not rely on context from headings or nearby images or text. While this approach might be okay for visual readers, it doesn’t work well for anyone relying on a machine reading code, like a screen reader.

And here’s what the rest of the page was exploring.

And then the first of the Tell me more and Get a quote links. You can see why our screen readers don’t know that they’re referring to Home insurance or Car insurance.

Links with the same name go to the same place.

As you saw, all the "get quote" links actually went to different pages because they related to different insurance types. Conversely links with different names should go to different places. don’t call one link “Phone us” and another link “Email us” if they’re both going to the contact us page.

Links are easy to see.

We mentioned this in the last session, but you need to make sure that you can spot links amidst body text. While this isn’t something you may be able to change, you can point out to your design partners.

The other thing to note here is consistent use of link styles. You don’t have to stick to just underlining links in every instance. But having 4 or 5 different link styles on the same page goes against the principle of consistency.

Keep underlined text for links.

Part 3: Images

Let’s look at the four different types of images.

Type 1: Decorative images

How are they defined?

The image could be removed from the page without removing any information.

Accessibility needs?

Should be ignored by assistive technologies.

Is the girl with the balloon decorative?

Is the tile a decorative image?

Yes, it's a duplicate.

Alt text?
  • On a CMS, mark as 'decorative'.
  • In code, provide a 'blank' or 'null' alt attribute.

<img src="example.png" alt="">

Type 2: Informative images

How are they defined?

The image plays an important part in understanding the overall story.

Accessibility needs?

Should include a text alternative that conveys the meaning or intent of the image.

Example of informative image

<img src="example.jpg"
      alt="Emma Bombonato, Manager of 
      Environmental Sustainability, 
      standing in front of the
      Sydney Opera House">

Is the woman in this picture informative?

Images that contain text need to be treated as informative images.

The alt text needs to include the same information as the image.

<img src="example.jpg"
    alt="Cartoon figure winking and 
    giving a peace sign. Text reads 
    'Good days require good sleep.'">

Context and alt text

A nearly empty Harvard Stadium.

What would you use for the alt text if the image was on a page about recent track try-outs?

Harvard Stadium with a man running up stone steps.

What would you use for the alt text if the image was on a page about stadium rennovations?

Harvard Stadium with cracked concrete pillars.

Alt text tips
  • Consider purpose and context
  • Keep descriptions concise
  • Prioritise information
  • Use plain language

Type 3: Functional images

How are they defined?

Images that are positioned inside links or buttons.

Accessibility needs?

Should include a text alternative that describes their function.

  • The function of the link: where it will take you?
  • The function of the button: what it will do?

Example of functional image


            <button>
              <img src="a.png" alt="Dismiss">
            </button>

Logos are a common example of functional images on web pages.

Check their web page?

Type 4: Complex images

How are they defined?

Images such as graphs, charts, maps, diagrams, infographics.

Accessibility needs?
  1. A short text alternative.
  2. A longer text description - which can be presented onscreen or on a new page.

Example of complex image

Alt text should identify the image and locate the longer description.

<img src="example.jpg"
    alt="Designing for Web Accessibility infographic.
  Detailed description below.">

We avoid long descriptions within the alt text, as this can be too verbose for assistive technologies.

Complex images should also be described on the same page, or made available on a different page via a link near the image.

Prioritisation

How can you determine which accessibility issues should take priority over others?

One of the most important ways is to determine how the issue impacts one or more types of users ability to accomplish a task.

A task could include:
  • Filling in a form.
  • Navigating to a location.
  • Accessing content.
  • Even choosing a date from a date picker.
Rating Explanation
Critical Stops one or more types of users from completing a task.
High

May cause serious problems or significant inconvenience to one or more types of users.

There must be at least one method available that allows these users to complete the task - even if it requires additional time.
Moderate May cause problems or frustrations for one or more types of users.
Low May be an inconvenience or irritation to one or more types of users.
