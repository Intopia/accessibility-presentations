Why should you care about accessibility?

Key reasons to care:
  1. Reduce legal risk
  2. Reduce reputational risk
  3. Avoid costly rework
  4. Improve user experience
  5. Create commercial opportunities

1. Reduce legal risk

All public-facing digital products in Australia must meet legal accessibility requirements.

Failing to do so could lead to complaints and even legal action.

Here are two key resources for understanding and meeting digital accessibility obligations in Australia:

  • The Disability Discrimination Act 1992
  • The Australian Human Rights Commission’s Digital Access Guidelines
The Disability Discrimination Act 1992

All public-facing digital products in Australia are required to comply with the ‘Disability Discrimination Act 1992’.

It is unlawful for a person who, whether for payment or not, provides goods or services, or makes facilities available, to discriminate against another person on the ground of the other person’s disability.

Source

Australian Human Rights Commission

The Commission recently released the Guidelines on Equal Access to Digital Goods and Services.

These guidelines offer practical advice on making websites, apps, and other digital services accessible to people with disability.

Organisations should conform with WCAG 2.2 at a minimum Level AA and consider appropriate Level AAA success criteria such as video transcripts, audio contrast, clear links and section headings.

Source

Have there been any legal cases?

Yes! A number of legal cases have highlighted what can happen when organisations fail to meet these responsibilities.

Some examples:
  • 2000: Bruce Maguire v. SOCOG
  • 2013: Haraksin v Murrays Australia
  • 2015: Gisele Mesnage v. Coles

2. Reduce reputational risk

Legal action can be costly, but the damage to an organisation’s reputation could be even worse.

2000: Bruce Maguire v SOCOG

Became a global symbol of inaccessible websites, reputational damage far outweighed small legal costs.

2013: Haraksin v Murrays Australia

National headlines highlighted failure to provide accessible coaches.

2015: Gisele Mesnage v Coles

Mainstream media coverage painted Coles as excluding customers with disabilities.

3. Avoid costly rework

Accessibility problems can increase the risk of costly rework when issues are found late in a project.

Fixing accessibility issues after launch is far more expensive and time-consuming than addressing them during design and development.

Embedding accessibility early helps teams work more efficiently and reduces rework costs.

4. Improve user experience

Features designed for people with disabilities often improve the user experience for other groups.

We’ve all heard the cliché about ramps into buildings.

They were originally built for wheelchair users, but they also help people with strollers, trolleys, or other mobility needs.

But here are some digital examples.

Example 1. Captions
  • Help people who are deaf or hard of hearing.
  • Also help people in noisy environments or with temporary hearing loss.
Example 2. Good contrast
  • Helps people with low vision or colour vision deficiency.
  • Also helps people viewing screens in bright light or experiencing temporary vision issues.
Example 3. Keyboard accessible
  • Helps people with no vision and people with limited movement.
  • Also helps people with temporary movement issues, people whose hands are busy, and power users.

5. Create commercial opportunities

Do you know the percentage of people who have some form of disability in Australia?

Some stats
  • Australia: 21.4% of the population
  • New Zealand: 24.3% of the population
  • USA: 26% of the population

These estimates are known to be low, as people:

  • May not identify themselves as having a disability.
  • May not want to disclose information on their disability.
  • May not be aware that they have a disability.

So the real number is likely higher — about 1 in 5 Australians.

Having an accessible website won’t magically increase your audience by one-fifth. The reality is far more nuanced.

However, every accessibility improvement removes barriers that prevent people from using your products.

Over time, those changes can potentially expand your reach and improve customer satisfaction.

People and assistive technologies

We’re going to look at a range of different people and how they interact with the digital world.

  1. People with no vision
  2. People with reduced vision
  3. People with reduced colour vision
  4. People with reduced movement
  5. People with varied learning or cognition
  6. People with reduced or no hearing

1. People with no vision

May include:
  • Blindness
  • Legally blind
May use:
  • A keyboard - without a mouse
  • Screen reader software (e.g. JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, Narrator)
  • Refreshable braille devices (e.g. Stand-alone, Smart displays)

2. People with reduced vision

May include:
  • Lens Disorders (e.g. Cataracts)
  • Retinal Disorders (e.g. Diabetic Retinopathy)
  • Optic Nerve Disorders (e.g. Glaucoma)
  • Corneal Disorders (e.g. Keratoconus)
  • Refractive and Developmental Disorders (e.g. Astigmatism)
May use:
  • System settings (e.g. magnification, font size, spacing, colour)
  • Browser-based page zoom (e.g. increase to 400%)
  • Increase text-size only (e.g. increase text to 200%)
  • Screen magnifier software (e.g. ZoomText)
  • Screen reader software (e.g. JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, Narrator)

3. People with reduced colour vision

Colour vision deficiency (CVD) or Colour blindness, is the decreased ability to see colour or differences in colour.

May include:
  • Green-deficient (Deuteranomaly) and Green-Blind (Deuteranopia)
  • Red-deficient (Protanomaly) and Red-Blind (Protanopia)
  • Blue-deficient (Tritanomaly) and Blue-Blind (Tritanopia)
  • Blue Cone Monochromacy (Achromatomaly)
  • Monochromacy (Achromatopsia)
May use:
  • Colour identification apps (e.g. Seeing AI, TapTapSee, Color Inspector)
  • Specialised software (e.g. Visolve)
  • Colour-corrective glasses: (e.g. EnChroma)

4. People with reduced movement

May include:
  • Traumatic injuries (e.g. Spinal cord injuries, loss of limbs)
  • Full-body conditions (e.g. Spina Bifida, Cerebral Palsy, Multiple Sclerosis)
  • Arm & hand conditions (e.g. Osteoarthritis, Rheumatoid Arthritis)
  • Other motor-related conditions (e.g. Tremors, Tic disorders)
May use:
  • Head pointers
  • Mouthsticks
  • Sip and puff devices
  • Switches
  • Eye or head tracking software (e.g. Dynavox, Apple Head Tracking)
  • ‘Speech-to-text’ software (e.g. Dragon NaturallySpeaking, Apple Dictation, Windows Voice Access)

5. People with varied learning or cognition

May include:
  • Intellectual disabilities (e.g. Down Syndrome, Fragile X Syndrome)
  • Neurodiverse conditions (e.g. Dyslexia, Dyscalculia, Autism Spectrum Disorder/Condition, Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder)
  • Processing differences (e.g. Aphasia, Auditory processing disorders)
  • Memory impairments (e.g. Dementia, Alzheimer disease)
May use:
  • ‘Text-to-speech’ software (e.g. Microsoft Immersive Reader, Apple Spoken Content)
  • ‘Speech-to-text’ software (e.g. Dragon NaturallySpeaking, Apple Dictation, Windows Voice Access)
  • Screen masking software (e.g. Read&Write)
  • Screen reader software (e.g. JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, Narrator)

6. People with reduced or no hearing

May include:
  • Hard of Hearing
  • Deaf
May use:
  • Hearing aids
  • Hearing or induction loops
  • ‘Speech-to-text’ software (e.g. Dragon NaturallySpeaking, Apple Dictation, Windows Voice Access)
  • Text messaging and instant messaging apps

Disabilities are varied and complex

  • Some people have multiple disabilities or conditions.
  • Disabilities may be spectrums.
  • Some disabilities change over time.
  • Some disabilities change from day to day.

Your customers

Any of these people could be your customers — now or in the future.
