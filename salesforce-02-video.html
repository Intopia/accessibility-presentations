Accessible Video

Video is a fantastic tool for delivering information and holding user attention.

But when we create content that requires a particular sense to acquire information, such as hearing or vision, we need to provide an alternative means of getting the same information.

Common media alternatives for videos include captions, transcripts, and audio descriptions.

Providing alternative formats means a person can choose the format that's right for them.

We also need to take some other design considerations, like audio quality and visual clarity, into consideration for accessibility.

Captions

Captions are a synchronized text version of all audio information needed to understand content.

Captions should contain all spoken dialogue, as well as all other important information such as relevant sounds (like a phone ringing) or music cues.

As a best practice, identifying changes in the current speaker is also recommended.

When media is live, captions are usually produced in real time by professional real time captioners.

For pre-recorded content, there are two types of captions.

The first is closed captions. These can be turned on and off by people who are watching the video.

The second is open captions, which are burned into the media file and thus always on.

We recommend closed captions, as they are more easily customisable and can be turned off by people who don’t need or want them.

They are better for content that will be translated into another language, and can be used to create a transcript more easily.

As well as people who are Deaf or hard of hearing, captions can benefit a range of other people.

This can include people with audio processing delays, non-native speakers, and people watching video with no sound.

Controls for turning captions on and off should be easy to find and use in the media player for all users.

Ensure your media player controls pass colour contrast and can be easily seen and understood.

Test your media player to make sure these controls are accessible by all inputs, such as keyboard and voice control, and remain available at all levels of zoom.

Transcripts

A transcript is a text version of audio and/or visual information needed to understand content.

It should contain all spoken content, as well as ideally any other audio or visual information required to understand the video.

This might include the sound of a phone ringing, the description of a painting, or the transcription of text that appears on screen.

Transcripts are useful for people who are Deaf or hard of hearing, blind, Deafblind, or who have cognitive or visual limitations.

However, there are many more people who will use transcripts.

These might include people short on time, people looking for a verbatim quote, or people translating the video into another language.

When providing a transcript, we must ensure it is located in close proximity to the media it relates to, is labelled clearly, and is in an accessible format.

And when a video has no sound, we should identify this somewhere on the page in close proximity to the video.

That way, people will know to look for the transcript straight away if needed.

For example, the Connections 2025 Keynote on Salesforce contains a transcript that can be accessed via the ‘Show Transcript’ button located immediately after the ‘Search’ button on the player.

Example: The Connections 2025 Keynote video on Salesforce+

Audio description

An alternative to providing a description of visual information in a transcript is to provide that description as an audio track.

This practice is usually called audio description.

Audio description benefits people who are blind or have reduced vision, or who aren’t looking at the video for any reason.

Audio description can be provided in two ways.

The first method is an audio narration.

A narrator will describe relevant visual information in gaps in the video audio.

This is usually provided as a separate audio track, but may also be integrated into the main video, or provided as a separate video.

We should let viewers know where and how any separate audio description is available, in close proximity to the video.

The second method is to integrate audio description into the video content.

For example, a presenter naturally describes what a viewer will see on screen, as part of the presentation.

This means a separate audio track is unnecessary.

Integrated audio description would be a simple method of ensuring this content is available to all viewers.

Sign language translation

You may also be asked to provide a sign language translation.

This involves a sign language interpreter either included as part of the video, or as a small overlay on-screen.

As a sign language interpreter must be large enough to be seen, ensure enough space in the frame is left for their inclusion if you know you will be providing this alternative.

Keep in mind that sign language is local.

An ASL (American Sign Language) interpretation for example may not be usable in another country.

Salesforce includes ASL translators at live major events.

Other considerations

As well as media alternatives, when creating video we need to keep several audio and visual considerations in mind for accessibility.

For audio, we need to ensure all audio is high quality so it can be easily discerned.

We want to ensure any background music or noise is at a low decibel, to help people with hearing or cognitive impairments to more easily understand the foreground audio.

Background audio should preferably be at least 20 decibels lower than foreground audio, or able to be turned off.

Any speech should be even-paced and clear, and should use plain language.

For visual content, we need to consider readability of text, such as our font and font size.

We must ensure any colours used pass colour contrast, and that we don’t use colour alone to convey meaning.

It’s also important that we avoid causing migraines and seizures.

Do not use flashing or strobing content, or warn viewers if this is included.

When embedding videos, it is not recommended that they autoplay.

This can delay and frustrate users who are using assistive technology, or who have cognitive disabilities.

For example, there are several autoplaying videos on this Dreamforce page.

While these videos can be paused, it would be better to avoid autoplaying content in general.

Conclusion

Accessible video doesn’t just benefit people with disabilities.

Many people watch video with the sound off, in a second language, or while distracted and not looking at the screen.

When reviewing your videos, ask yourself, “Do I need all senses to understand what this video is about?”

Particularly consider how someone with no hearing or no vision watching your content would experience it, and whether the alternatives provided meet their needs.
