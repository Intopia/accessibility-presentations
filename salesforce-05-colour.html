Designing accessibly with colour

Our priority with visual design is to ensure that information is one, easy to perceive, and two, easy to understand.

Colour plays a major role in visual design.

It can support your brand message, grab a person's attention, or influence their mood.

But not everyone can see colour in the same way.

We want to ensure colour has appropriate contrast, and isn’t relied on to convey information.

We also want our visual designs to be customisable for those whose needs aren’t met by our designs.

Colour contrast

Using colours with good contrast benefits people who have reduced contrast sensitivity.

Contrast refers to the difference in brightness between the foreground or primary colour, and the background or adjacent colour.

People may have difficulty with contrast sensitivity because of:

  • An eye condition.
  • A cognitive condition (such as migraines).
  • The environment (such as low light or glare).
  • As a result of the natural aging process.

When we measure the contrast between two colours, the contrast is expressed as a ratio along a range between 1:1 and 21:1.

For example, if we measure the contrast of white on white, the contrast ratio is 1:1. This is the lowest possible contrast ratio.

These colours would be impossible to see against each other.

By comparison, if we measure the contrast of black on white, the contrast ratio is 21:1 - the highest possible contrast ratio.

WCAG sets a minimum contrast ratio that colour must meet in order to be considered generally perceivable.

For meaningful text, this is:

  • 4.5:1 for regular font (smaller than 24px, or 19px bold)
  • 3:1 for large font (equal to or larger than 24px, or 19px bold)

This does not apply to:

  • Text that is part of programmatically inactive components.
  • Decorative elements.
  • Logos.

Meaningful non-text visual information must have a colour contrast of at least: 3:1.

This includes:

  • Graphics.
  • Icons.
  • States (focus, hover, checked, selected etc).
  • Outlines for user interface components (borders for form fields and buttons).

Non-text contrast exceptions include:

  • Logos.
  • Default browser elements.
  • Programmatically disabled components.

Unique backgrounds and foregrounds

When testing colour contrast between the foreground and background, this includes text that is laid over an image.

If an image contains multiple colours, any significant swatches of colour must be tested separately against the text.

For example, check out this ‘Nothing here yet’ banner. We must test the white text against each major part of the background.

So while the text passes contrast against the blue sky, we run into problems when comparing it to the face of our mascot.

Example: https://www.salesforce.com/plus/mylist

Gradients represent a unique challenge for colour contrast.

Measurements of contrast between all points of low contrast is required at multiple viewport sizes.

This is because gradients in foreground text will likely scale differently on a desktop viewport compared to a mobile browser viewport.

Contrast for image backgrounds can often be improved by adding a semi-transparent overlay on the image to lighten its colour and improve contrast.

Alternatively, text surrounded by a drop shadow will have increased contrast.

If a tile changes colour due to receiving focus, both colours must be tested against the text.

Colour and meaning

Because some people cannot perceive all colours, or perceive colours differently, colour should never be used as the only visual means of:

  • Conveying information.
  • Indicating or prompting an action.
  • Distinguishing a visual element.

The following are a few examples of where we commonly see this occur:

Forms may use colour as the only means to indicate required fields.

Status updates may use colour to signal essential content – i.e if something passes or fails.

Links could have underlines turned off so that colour is the only method of distinguishing links from surrounding text.

Charts and diagrams may use colour as the only means of communicating key information.

These examples aren't good practice, as anyone who cannot distinguish the colour may miss out on receiving that information.

For example, on the Trailhead Academy course selection page, colourful document icons convey what stream the course corresponds to.

This information is not conveyed in any other way on this page.

Problematic example: The document icons on the course options on https://trailheadacademy.salesforce.com/home have different colours but no other indication of what that means (I think it refers to roles - see https://trailheadacademy.salesforce.com/catalog)

To prevent this happening, we should not use colour as the sole method to convey information.

This doesn’t mean don’t use colour at all – but that we should include a secondary way of conveying the same information.

For example: include a text label of “required” as well as using colour.

Include text or symbols in a status update as well as colour.

Ensure links have underlines as well as colour.

Provide text labels to identify data on charts.

On this Salesforce pricing table, a green tick has been used to indicate a product’s inclusion.

This uses two different methods of conveying the same information, colour and symbol.

Example: https://www.salesforce.com/sales/pricing/

Use tables to demonstrate use of colour and symbols together.

Customization

We can improve content accessibility for many users by following these general guidelines.

However, many of our users may still need unique settings to make text more accessible to them.

For example, they may want to change text size, colour, or even font, to make it easier for them to read.

To support this, we should use real text whenever possible, not text embedded in images.

When a user applies custom settings to a page, such as turning on High Contrast Mode, they need every part of the page to respond to that setting.

When text is provided as an image, it cannot be customised and will not respond to any user settings.

This means the text may be difficult or impossible for a person to read, and could also trigger migraines or visual disturbances.

For instances where we must provide text embedded in an image (such as an infographic or chart), we can support these users by providing the same information as a real text alternative.

This means that all the information in the image can be customized and read through a secondary means.

For example, these statistics on the Salesforce website are real text. This means a user can change their appearance to suit their needs.

Example: See big statistics tiles near bottom of https://www.salesforce.com/customer-stories/agentforce-for-HR-IT/

Conclusion

Colour is a significant consideration in accessible design.

Ensuring everyone can perceive and understand our content will greatly improve our accessibility.

We also want to support user customisation, for when a user needs to make a few extra tweaks.
