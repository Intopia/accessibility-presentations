Why should you care about accessibility?

Key reasons to care:
  • Commercial incentives
  • Reputation
  • Corporate Social Responsibility
  • Legal responsibilities

Commercial incentives

Question:

Do you know the percentage of people in Australia who have some form of disability?

In Australia, the term “disability” means:

"Any limitation, restriction or impairment which restricts everyday activities and has lasted, or is likely to last, for at least six months."

In 2018 the Australian Bureau of Statistics reported that there were 4.4 million Australians with a disability or 17.7% of the population.

And the prevalence of disability increased with age;

  • 11.6% of people aged 0-64 years had a disability
  • 49.6% of people aged 65 years and over had a disability

It is well-known that these estimates may be low, as many people do not identify themselves as having some form of disability.

Globally, people with disabilities control around $8 trillion in spending.

Can you afford to lose potential or existing customers due to inaccessible products and services?

Reputation

In 2015, the legal case Gisele Mesnage vs Coles was settled out of court.

While a financial risk is associated with legal actions like this, the possible damage to an organisation's reputation is a far greater risk.

If you receive a complaint via social media or any other channel, the manner and speed of your response could have a massive impact on the end result.

Question:

Should you get an accessibility complaint about any of your products and services, do you have and guidelines or policies in place?

Corporate Social Responsibility

Corporate social responsibility (CSR) is a business model that helps companies be aware of their economic, social, and environmental impacts.

Diversity, equity, inclusion and accessibility should be part of any companies Corporate social responsibility model.

This includes how you engage with the public, who you employ, who you work with and how you deliver your products and services.

A solid CSR will help to increase customer and staff loyalty as well as improve workforce diversity.

It has been shown that diversity and inclusion within teams can lead to improved team performance.

Legal responsibilities

Question:

Do you know the rules around public-facing digital products within Australia?

All public-facing digital products in Australia are required to comply with the Disability Discrimination Act 1992.

"The provision of online services through the web is a service covered by the DDA. Equal access for people with a disability is required by the DDA where it can reasonably be provided. This requirement applies to any organisation developing a website in Australia."

Australian Human Rights Commission - Disability Discrimination Act Advisory Notes v4.1

What is WCAG?

Question:

What does the term "WCAG" stand for?

The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) is the main organisation that develops international standards for the World Wide Web.

One of the standards produced by the W3C is the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines - often referred to as “WCAG”.

WCAG is the worldwide standard for web accessibility. It explains how to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities.

The latest version of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines is Version 2.1.

WCAG is structured in the following way:

  • 4 Principles
    • 13 Guidelines
      • 78 Success Criteria
        • Sufficient Techniques
        • Advisory Techniques
        • Failures

While many people focus on checking off success criteria, understanding and following the four overall principles is more important.

The four principles are:
  • Perceivable
  • Operable
  • Understandable
  • Robust

Perceivable

Information and user interface components must be presentable to users in ways they can perceive.

For example, you cannot provide content or UI functionality that is not available to people with loss of hearing or loss of sight.

Operable

User interface components and navigation must be operable.

For example, you cannot build a website or application that relies on users having the ability to use the mouse.

Understandable

Information and the operation of the user interface must be understandable.

For example, you cannot build UI functionality that alienates people who cannot understand the full context.

Robust

Content must be robust enough that a wide variety of technologies can access it.

This means that users must be able to access the content as technologies advance.

Roles and responsibilities

You may have heard the term "shift left". This is about making sure that accessibility is considered as early as possible in the process.

When responsibilities for accessibility are not clearly defined and communicated, they are often left until too late in the lifecycle.

For example, developers could be left to address issues like color selection and heading identification that are not their responsibility.

Often these responsibilities should have been addressed by other roles earlier in the process, such as user experience designers, visual designers, and content authors.

These issues can lead to tech and/or design dept, as well as impacting on project timelines and budgets.

It is important to understand accessibility responsibilities for roles such as UX and UI designers, developers, testers and content creators.

The ABC has a great visual resource for teams: Accessibility. Tips for teams

Intopia also has a resource that includes detailed information about roles and responsibilities: Accessibility (Not-)Checklist.

These documents can help teams to identify who should address accessibility issues and then create the appropriate processes.

Beyond WCAG

To be accessible, teams need to look at the overall User Experience, not just WCAG conformance.

Within your company, what degree of user experience do you aim for?

Technically accessible:

Is it technically possible for users to access the information or perform the steps needed to accomplish the task?

Usable:

Are users able to effectively and efficiently complete tasks?

Satisfying/enjoyable:

Are users satisfied with the experience; is the experience enjoyable (if it is supposed to be)?

You are probably already conducting user research on your products - using real users to provide valuable insights.

One simple addition would be to include people with disabilities within the user research process.

This will allow you to identify potential problems early in the process and potentially resolve these issues well before production.

Testing for accessibility

There are a wide range of automated testing tools that can be used to identify and address accessibility issues.

Automated desktop tools
  • Axe by Deque
  • Microsoft Accessibility Insights
  • ANDI tool
  • WAVE tool
Automated mobile tools
  • iOS Accessibility Inspector
  • Android Accessibility Scanner
  • Axe for Android
  • Android Studio - Lint

There are also many manual testing tools available that can be used to identify specific accessibility issues.

Assistance tools
  • Web Developer extension
  • HeadingMaps
Colour and contrast tools
  • Colour Contrast Analyser by TPGI
  • Contrast Ratio by Lea Verou
  • WebAIM Color Contrast Checker
  • Tanaguru contrast finder
  • Stark
Desktop screen readers
  • JAWS for Windows (licensed)
  • NVDA for Windows
  • VoiceOver for MacOS
Mobile screen readers
  • TalkBack (Android)
  • VoiceOver for iOS (Apple)
Desktop Speech Interfaces
  • Dragon NaturallySpeaking (licensed)
  • Voice Control (Mac OS)
Mobile Speech Interfaces
  • Voice Access (Android)
  • Voice Control (iOS)

While these tools can be beneficial for teams, they should not replace expert reviews, accessibility audits or inclusive user research and testing.

What next?

1. Begin with awareness training across the organisation

2. Build capabilities and understanding within your team.

3. Include people with disabilities in key processes.

4. Introduce manual and automated testing into processes.

5. Begin regular audits of products.

6. Regularly review and evaluate accessibility principles, guidelines and practices.
