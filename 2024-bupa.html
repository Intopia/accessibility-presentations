Legal responsibilities

All public-facing digital products in Australia are required to comply with the ‘Disability Discrimination Act 1992’.

It is unlawful for a person who, whether for payment or not, provides goods or services, or makes facilities available, to discriminate against another person on the ground of the other person’s disability.

Source

The Australian Human Rights Commission recently released the Guidelines on Equal Access to Digital Goods and Services.

These guidelines offer practical advice on making websites, apps, and other digital services accessible to people with disability.

Organisations should conform with WCAG 2.2 at a minimum Level AA and consider appropriate Level AAA success criteria such as video transcripts, audio contrast, clear links and section headings.

Source

A number of legal cases have highlighted what can happen when organisations fail to meet these responsibilities.

Some examples:
  • 2000: Bruce Maguire v. SOCOG
  • 2013: Haraksin v Murrays Australia
  • 2015: Gisele Mesnage v. Coles

What is WCAG?

The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) develops international standards for the World Wide Web.

One of these standards is the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines - often shortened to ‘WCAG’.

WCAG explains how to make websites and digital content more accessible to people with disabilities.

The current standard is WCAG 2.2, released on 5 October 2023.

WCAG structure

WCAG is organised into layers, from high-level principles through to detailed examples:

  • Principles
    • Guidelines
      • Success Criteria
        • Sufficient Techniques
        • Advisory Techniques
        • Failures

Principles

These are the foundations of accessibility, ensuring content can be used from multiple perspectives.

They are often remembered by the acronym POUR.

  • Perceivable: content can be seen, heard, or otherwise sensed
  • Operable: users can interact with it in different ways
  • Understandable: information and operation are clear and predictable
  • Robust: works reliably with current and future technologies, including assistive tech

Guidelines

Each principle is broken down into 12 guidelines.

These are goals that point teams in the right direction but are not directly testable on their own.

Success Criteria

Each guideline contains testable Success Criteria. These are the measurable checks for accessibility.

In WCAG 2.2, there are 87 Success Criteria in total.

Compliance levels

Each Success Criterion has one of three levels:

Level A

Minimum requirements that remove the most critical barriers for some people with disabilities.

Level AA

The recommended target for most organisations. Balances accessibility with practical implementation.

Level AAA

The highest level. Includes advanced accessibility improvements, but not all content can reasonably meet this level.

To be “AA compliant”, a site must meet all Level A and Level AA criteria — a total of 56 criteria.

Some important WCAG documents

The following document is normative - an official requirement.

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines 2.2

The official list of Success Criteria. These are the requirements for WCAG conformance.

The following documents are informative - helpful guidance.

All WCAG 2.2 Understanding Docs

Plain-language explanations of each criterion: what it means, why it matters, and how to apply it.

How to Meet WCAG (Quick Reference)

An interactive checklist where you can filter by role, technology, or topic to find the criteria and techniques that apply to your work.

A tool to help?

Accessibility Not-Checklist (BETA)

A tool that lets you sort and filter WCAG success criteria and best practices by role, topic, and project phase.

It’s about people

In late 2020, I was working at an insurance company when someone in upper management asked a question:

Are people with disabilities likely to use our products? Do we need to worry about these people?

To try and give this person some perspective, I interviewed 5 different people to ask them about their lives and needs.

  • 3 people with no vision
  • 1 person with reduced vision
  • 1 person with reduced movement

All of them do their own banking, shopping and insurance renewals.

All of them talked about some key desires: independence and privacy.

They all talked about how they wanted to be able to do their own tasks.

And all of them talked about being frustrated when they needed help to complete any of these processes.

Accessibility is about giving people freedom and independence - regardless of the product or service.
