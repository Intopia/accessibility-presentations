Why should you care about accessibility?

Key reasons to care:
  1. Legal responsibilities
  2. Reputation
  3. Improved user experience
  4. Commercial incentives

Legal responsibilities

There are three key resources for understanding and meeting digital accessibility obligations in Australia:

  1. The Disability Discrimination Act 1992
  2. The Australian Human Rights Commission’s Digital Access Guidelines
  3. The AS EN 301 549 Accessibility Standard

1. All public-facing digital products in Australia are required to comply with the ‘Disability Discrimination Act 1992’.

It is unlawful for a person who, whether for payment or not, provides goods or services, or makes facilities available, to discriminate against another person on the ground of the other person’s disability.

Source

2. The Australian Human Rights Commission recently released the Guidelines on Equal Access to Digital Goods and Services.

These guidelines help organisations understand how to meet their obligations under the Disability Discrimination Act.

They offer practical advice on making websites, apps, and other digital services accessible to people with disability.

Organisations should conform with WCAG 2.2 at a minimum Level AA and consider appropriate Level AAA success criteria such as video transcripts, audio contrast, clear links and section headings.

Source

3. The AS EN 301 549 Accessibility Standard defines accessibility requirements for ICT products and services.

This standard covers:

  • Websites and mobile apps
  • Software and operating systems
  • Computers, tablets, and smartphones
  • Self-service kiosks, ATMs, ticket machines
  • Digital documents (e.g. PDFs, Word files)
  • Online platforms and cloud services

This standard supports legal compliance with the Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (DDA).

A number of legal cases have highlighted what can happen when organisations fail to meet these responsibilities.

Some examples:
  • 2019, USA: Guillermo Robles v. Domino’s Pizza
  • 2015, Australia: Gisele Mesnage v. Coles
  • 2000, Australia: Bruce Maguire v. SOCOG

Reputation

Legal action can be costly, but the damage to an organisation’s reputation could be even worse.

No organisation wants to end up in the headlines for the wrong reasons:

Domino’s will likely forever be remembered as the brand that argued against the basic rights of a blind man - and lost.

Source

Coles has agreed to improve its online shopping website so that it can be more easily accessed by blind people, after being taken to court for alleged disability discrimination.

Source

Improved user experience

Features designed for people with disabilities often improve the user experience for many other groups.

Inclusive design also supports people with temporary or situational constraints.

Examples of temporary constraints:
  • Temporary hearing loss - affecting ability to hear audio.
  • Eye infection - affecting vision.
  • Sprained wrist - affecting ability to type.
  • Migraine - affecting ability to focus or read.
Examples of situational constraints:
  • Bright sunlight – hard to see the screen.
  • Noisy café – hard to hear video or audio.
  • Holding a baby – unable to use a mouse.
  • Wearing gloves – unable to use a touchscreen.

Commercial incentives

Do you know the percentage of people who have some form of disability in Australia?

Some stats
  • Australia: 21.4% of the population
  • New Zealand: 24.3% of the population
  • USA: 26% of the population

These estimates are known to be low, as people:

  • May not identify themselves as having a disability.
  • May not want to disclose information on their disability.
  • May not be aware that they have a disability.

So the real number is likely higher — about 1 in 5 Australians.

Why should you care?

Accessible products will help:
  • Reduce legal risk.
  • Attract more visitors.
  • Improve customer experience.
  • Position your organisation as an inclusive, caring brand.

Accessibility user profiles

How can we plan, design and build for all the different types of disabilities?

One solution is to create a set of user profiles that follow the broad categories we just reviewed.

  1. A person with no vision
  2. A person with low vision
  3. A person with reduced colour vision
  4. A person with limited movement
  5. A person with varied learning or cognition
  6. A person with reduced or no hearing
Claudia: low vision
Key info:

Claudia is a 54-year-old social worker with low vision.

Devices and technology:

Claudia uses ZoomText, a large monitor and a high-visibility keyboard.

She changes default colours to increase contrast.

She uses the ‘speech to text’ feature on her smartphone.

Frustrations:

Claudia tends to forget to scroll horizontally when using ZoomText.

She sometimes misses form fields or help text when they’re next to each other rather than stacked.

She hates it when pop-up boxes appear off-screen, as she needs to scroll to find and close the pop-up.

They allow teams to focus on:
  • People rather than compliance.
  • Different types of people and their individual needs.
  • One profile at a time.
These profiles should not replace:
  • Expert reviews
  • Accessibility audits
  • Inclusive user research and testing

When should these profiles be used?

These profiles have the most significant impact when used during the early stages of design, development and testing processes.

For example, how would each of these user profiles engage and interact with the following scenarios?

  • A user-journey (e.g. Getting an insurance quote)
  • A process (e.g. Making a payment)
  • A page (e.g. Filling in a form)
  • A component (e.g. Choosing a date from a date picker)

An example scenario:

Imagine each of these 6 profiles interacting with a date picker. Would any of them have specific needs?

A person with varied learning or cognition

Do they understand the purpose of the field from the label, instructions and UI?

A person with low vision

Can they interact with the field if viewed in high contrast or magnified?

A person with limited movement

Can they interact with all features within the date picker using keyboard-only?

A person with no vision

Will each aspect be correctly announced to screen readers?

Accessibility in the workplace

Technology

Ensure all internal digital products meet accessibility standards (e.g. WCAG 2.2, EN 301 549).

Digital products:
  • Software
  • Intranets
  • Communication platforms
  • Internal systems
  • Documents and templates (e.g. Word, PDF, PowerPoint)
Accessibility includes:
  • Support screen readers
  • Support for customisation (e.g. increase text size, colour changes)
  • Sufficient colour contrast
  • Support for keyboard-only users
  • Captions and transcripts (for audio and video)

Test internal systems for compatibility with assistive tech during procurement and onboarding phases.

Provide assistive technologies where needed such as screen readers, speech-to-text, magnifiers, and alternative input devices.

Ensure IT support staff are trained in accessibility needs, including how to support users with different disabilities.

Enable flexibility in tech setups, including adjustable font sizes, contrast settings, and input methods.

Allow admin access to staff laptops where relevant, as some users need to customise their entire digital experience (e.g. installing specialist software or accessibility tools).

Physical Facilities

Provide step-free access to all areas including entrances, workspaces, meeting rooms, kitchens, and toilets.

Provide accessible parking spaces near building entrances and exits.

Install accessible signage with clear text, tactile elements, and good contrast.

Ensure accessible toilets and changing facilities are available, not used as storage, and regularly maintained.

Offer height-adjustable desks and adaptable workstations for varied physical needs.

Ensure lighting and acoustics support a range of sensory needs, including non-fluorescent lighting options.

Maintain clear walkways and accessible emergency exits, with alarms that include visual and tactile alerts.

Provide inclusive meeting spaces with accessible seating arrangements and hearing support systems (like induction loops).

Provide quiet spaces where people can decompress, supporting neurodivergent staff and others with sensory sensitivities.

Avoid hot-desking where relevant, as some staff need consistent seating, specific desk setups, or environmental adjustments.

Work arrangements

Offer flexibility in working arrangements, including remote work, flexible hours, or alternative communication methods.

Providing workplace adjustments

Here are some steps to help create for a more inclusive, accessible workplace.

Involve staff in the decision-making, rather than assuming what will work best for them.

Ask staff: “What do you need to do your best work?” in a private, respectful way.

Create a simple adjustments process that’s well-communicated and easy to use, with clear steps and quick responses.

Ensure managers are trained to recognise when adjustments might be needed and how to respond supportively and legally.

Give employees choice and control in tech and tools, such as selecting accessible software or hardware configurations.

Make adjustments proactive, not reactive, by building accessibility into default workspaces, tools, and processes.

Maintain confidentiality and psychological safety around adjustments, ensuring privacy and no fear of stigma.

Review adjustments regularly in check-ins or performance reviews, especially if roles, locations, or tools change.

Create feedback loops so staff can suggest improvements or flag inaccessible tools, facilities, or practices.

Collaborate with facilities, IT, and HR to ensure adjustments are joined-up across systems and spaces.

Document and share success stories (with permission) to normalise adjustments and demonstrate organisational commitment.
