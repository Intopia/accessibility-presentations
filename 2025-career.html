Legal responsibilities in different countries

Australia

There are three key resources for understanding and meeting digital accessibility obligations in Australia:

  1. The Disability Discrimination Act 1992
  2. The Australian Human Rights Commission’s Digital Access Guidelines
  3. The AS EN 301 549 Accessibility Standard

1. All public-facing digital products in Australia are required to comply with the ‘Disability Discrimination Act 1992’.

It is unlawful for a person who, whether for payment or not, provides goods or services, or makes facilities available, to discriminate against another person on the ground of the other person’s disability.

Source

2. The Australian Human Rights Commission recently released the Guidelines on Equal Access to Digital Goods and Services.

These guidelines help organisations understand how to meet their obligations under the Disability Discrimination Act.

They offer practical advice on making websites, apps, and other digital services accessible to people with disability.

Organisations should conform with WCAG 2.2 at a minimum Level AA and consider appropriate Level AAA success criteria such as video transcripts, audio contrast, clear links and section headings.

Source

3. The AS EN 301 549 Accessibility Standard defines accessibility requirements for ICT products and services.

This standard covers:

  • Websites and mobile apps
  • Software and operating systems
  • Computers, tablets, and smartphones
  • Self-service kiosks, ATMs, ticket machines
  • Digital documents (e.g. PDFs, Word files)
  • Online platforms and cloud services

This standard supports legal compliance with the Disability Discrimination Act 1992 (DDA).

USA

At the national level the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) applies to private and Government sites.

  • Guide to Disability Rights Laws

There is also Section 508 of the Rehabilitation Act that applies to ICT in the Federal Government.

  • About the ADA Accessibility Standards
  • IT Accessibility Laws and Policies

At the State level there are also various requirements.

  • Digital Accessibility Regulation in 2023: An Update
  • Section 508 by state

Canada

The Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act, 2005

Under the Information and Communications Standards, organizations must make their websites, including web-based apps, accessible.

Organizations must do so by making their websites compliant with Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.0, Level AA.

This international standard gives web developers guidelines on how to make their webpages accessible to computer users with disabilities. From: AODA Requirement for Ontario Websites

The European Union

The EU Accessibility Act 2025 is a landmark law that mandates a few everyday products and services to be easily accessible for specially-abled individuals.

  • European accessibility act

The EU and all Member States made this initiative upon ratifying the United Nations Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities.

With a deadline set for June 28, 2025, companies must act promptly to ensure compliance with the European Accessibility Act.

Declaring conformance includes:
  • Publishing an accessibility statement on your website
  • Completing a VPAT, VPAT 2.5 Rev EU (November 2023)
  • Referencing the VPAT in the accessibility statement
  • Must be provided in the language of each EU member state where the product is available
  • Must be continuously maintained
The accessibility statement must cover:
  • The functionality of the digital product
  • How accessibility is managed within an organization, such as:
    • Implementing accessibility training for employees
    • Monitoring accessibility on an ongoing basis
    • The names of enforcement authorities to which users can file accessibility complaints

The United Kingdom

One of the key pieces of web accessibility legislation in the UK is the Equality Act 2010 (EQA).

  • Overview of UK website accessibility laws

Under the wide-ranging EQA, UK goods and service providers have a legal obligation not to discriminate against people – including disability.

Intopia does not have expertise in the following countries:

China

Information technology - Technical requirements and testing methods for accessibility of Internet content (Ministry of Industry and Information Technology)

From what we understand, China prohibits businesses and government agencies from discriminating against individuals on the basis of disability.

The Law doesn’t include technical criteria for web accessibility, and it doesn’t mention WCAG.

However, China does have technical standards for web accessibility, and those standards are based on an early version of WCAG.

Hong Kong

Guidelines on Dissemination of Information through Government Websites, a publication of the Office of Government Chief Information Officer, Government of Hong Kong.

Those guidelines focus on Government websites only, and use WCAG 2.0 Level AA.

A common theme?

Most countries require some level of WCAG compliance.

What is the scope of digital accessibility?

Digital accessibility generally includes:

  • Websites and web Applications
  • Mobile Apps (Native & Hybrid)
  • Digital Documents
  • Video & Multimedia
  • E-commerce & Booking Systems
  • Email & Digital Marketing
  • Learning Management Systems (LMS)
  • Software applications

Physical accessibility generally includes:

  • Buildings & Entrances
  • Transport Systems
  • Kiosks & ATMs
  • Facilities & Amenities
  • Signage
  • Emergency Systems
  • Footpaths and Streetscapes
  • Parks and Outdoor Spaces

However, in some cases, the information presented on-screen for Kiosks & ATMs could also be classified as digital information.

Accessibility features such as colour contrast, structure, labels, target size, gestures etc would all be relevant.

This is especially relevant for touch screen interfaces.

People’s access needs and technology use

We generally focus on the following broad categories of disabilities:

  1. People with no vision
  2. People with reduced vision
  3. People with reduced colour vision
  4. People with reduced movement
  5. People with varied learning or cognition
  6. People with reduced or no hearing

Assistive technologies used by these different groups can include:

  • Screen reader software (e.g. JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, Narrator)
  • Refreshable braille devices (e.g. Stand-alone, Smart displays)
  • Head pointers
  • Mouthsticks
  • Sip and puff devices
  • Head switches
  • Eye or head tracking software
  • ‘Text-to-speech’ software (e.g. Microsoft Immersive Reader)
  • ‘Speech-to-text’ software (e.g. Dragon NaturallySpeaking)

Motor Impairments

Motor impairments encompass a range of conditions affecting movement and coordination.

Exact global figures are challenging to determine, as there is no way to track users and their technologies.

Vision Impairments

Over 2.2 billion people globally have some form of vision impairment.

At least 1 billion of these cases could have been prevented or are yet to be addressed.

Projections indicate that by 2050, the number of people with sight loss will increase to over 1.7 billion.

Hearing Impairments

More than 1.5 billion people (nearly 20% of the global population) live with hearing loss.

Of these, 430 million have disabling hearing loss.

By 2050, this number is expected to rise to over 700 million.

Memory Impairments (Dementia)

As of 2020, over 55 million people worldwide were living with dementia.

This number is projected to reach 78 million by 2030 and 139 million by 2050.

Neurodiversity (e.g., Autism, ADHD, Dyslexia)

Approximately 15–20% of the global population is considered neurodivergent.

Dyslexia affects around 9–12% of people worldwide.

Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) is estimated to affect 1–3% of the global population.

Screen Reader Usage

According to WebAIM's Screen Reader User Survey #10 (2024), which included 1,539 respondents:

  • NVDA: Used by 65.6% of respondents.
  • JAWS: Used by 60.5% of respondents.
  • VoiceOver (macOS/iOS): Used by 43.9% of respondents.
  • Narrator (Windows): Used by 37.3% of respondents.

ZoomText and Magnification Tools

In the same WebAIM survey, 7.5% of respondents reported using ZoomText/Fusion.

Additionally, a survey by Vision Australia found that users relying on zoom text and magnification tools reported more difficulty using websites compared to those using screen readers.

Motor Skill Assistive Technologies

While specific global usage statistics for motor skill assistive technologies are limited, the UK Government's 2016 assistive technology survey provides some insights:

  • Screen Magnifier: Used by 30% of respondents.
  • Screen Reader: Used by 29% of respondents.
  • Speech Recognition: Used by 18% of respondents.
  • Readability Tools: Used by 15% of respondents.
