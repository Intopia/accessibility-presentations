Types of disabilities

Over the years, I've noticed some common misconceptions around inclusion and disabilities.

  • Disabled = people that are blind.
  • Disabled = people in wheelchairs.

So, the aim of this next set of slides is to show you the breadth of the disability spectrum.

I’m going to move through these slides quickly, as the details of each disability are not critical at this time.

Disabilities are often grouped into five broad categories:

  • Visual.
  • Auditory.
  • Physical.
  • Cognitive, learning, and neurological.
  • Speech.

1. Visual

A. Low Vision:
  • Macular degeneration.
  • Glaucoma.
  • Diabetic retinopathy.
  • Cataract.
B. Colour Vision Deficiencies:

Monochromacy

  • Rod monochromacy: inability to distinguish colours.
  • Cone monochromacy: inability to distinguish colours.
B. Colour Vision Deficiencies (continued):

Dichromacy

  • Protanopia: absence of red retinal photoreceptors.
  • Deuteranopia: absence of red retinal photoreceptors.
  • Tritanopia: absence of blue retinal photoreceptors.
B. Colour Vision Deficiencies (continued):

Anomalous trichromacy:

  • Protanomaly: poor red–green hue discrimination.
  • Deuteranomaly: mildly affected red–green hue discrimination.
  • Tritanomaly: poor blue–green and yellow–red/pink hue discrimination.
Side note:

Although “colour blindness” is a commonly used term, it is inaccurate in most cases. “Colour blindness” only refers to people with Monochromacy.

C. Blindness
  • Blindness.
  • Legally blind.
Side note:

A person is considered legally blind if they:

  • Can’t see at six metres what someone with regular vision can see at 60 metres, or
  • Their field of vision is less than 20 degrees in diameter.
D. Other visual-related conditions
  • Photosensitive epilepsy.

2. Auditory

Auditory disabilities:
  • Hearing loss.
  • Hearing impaired.
  • Deafness.
Side note 1:

A small percentage of the deaf community that cannot read, and rely on sign language alone. For others, English is a second or third language.

Today, Asperger's syndrome is technically no longer a diagnosis on its own. It is now part of a broader category called autism spectrum disorder (ASD).

Side note 2:

Neurodiversity is the idea that people experience and interact with the world in many different ways; there is no one "right" way. Differences should not be viewed as deficits.

Disabilities are varied and complex

Disabilities are varied and changing:
  • Disabilities may be spectrums
  • Some people have multiple disabilities or conditions.
  • Some disabilities change over time.
  • Some disabilities change day to day.
People may experience temporary barriers:
  • A broken arm.
  • Temporary hearing loss.
  • Concussion.
People may experience situational barriers:
  • Unable to see a screen due to sunlight.
  • Unable to hear audio due to a local noise.
  • Unable to use a mouse while holding a baby.

Two big questions!

When you realise there are an incredibly diverse range of disabilities, it's easy to feel overwhelmed.

This often leads people to two big questions:

1. Why do we need to provide solutions for all of these different types of people? Especially if I only work in print media?

2. How could we ever plan, design, build for all these different types of people?

We’ll explore the importance of providing solutions for everyone soon, but Let’s start with "how".

One simple solution is to focus on user profiles or personas that can be used to represent different disability sets.

This means you can focus on a small set of user types, rather than a huge list of specific disabilities and impairments.

These user profiles or personas can then be used throughout ever stage of your process to ensure inclusion.

Accessibility considerations for print and PDF material

Language

  • Use plain language.
  • Avoid complex words, jargon, legal terms or acronyms
  • Always write for your intended target audience.
  • Use active voice.

Layout

  • Use clear and visible headings.
  • Use a clear heading hierarchy.
  • Place the most important information first.
  • Keep messages short and simple.
  • Avoid complex or busy layouts.
  • Make use of white space to improve readability.

Colour

  • Make sure foreground and background colours have sufficient colour contrast.

Design

  • Use sans-serif rather than serif for body content.
  • Use appropriate font size (minimum 12-14pt).
  • Avoid placing text over graphics.
  • Avoid using all-capitals for text.
  • Avoid centre-aligned text.

PDFs

On top of all previously mentioned checkpoints:

  • Does the PDF fully pass Adobe Accessibility Checker?
  • Is all content contained in tags?
  • Do the tags match the order they should be read in?
  • Can text be resized?
