2. Why include people with disabilities?

There are three main reasons to include people with disability in your research.

  1. To include a diversity of views. (Important for any process)
  2. To target a relevant demographic to understand their lived experience. (Interviews, Focus groups, User testing)
  3. To test specific assistive technology users across a process, page or component. (User testing)
  4. To conduct assessibility testing.

Some people accidentally confuse items 2 and 3 when setting up testing processes.

It is acceptable to include both approaches in a single session, but they have different purposes and outcomes.

For item 3, you are obverving how people use specific assistive technologies to interact with processes, pages or components.

User testing should never be confused with accessibility testing - conducted by experts.

All materials should be accessibility tested by experts before user testing.

People with lived experience may not be accessibility experts.

You are after their lived experience. It is not their job to identify accessibility issues.

3. Who should you include at different stages?

We are going to ask you which broad disability groups you would consider including during the following stages:

  1. “High level concept” phase
  2. “Design mockup” phase
  3. “Developed product” phase
High level concepts?

When testing high-level concepts in interviews or focus groups, which types of people with disabilities could be included?

Include:
  • Anyone!
Design mockups?

When testing design mockups (e.g. Figma files), which types of people with disabilities could be included? And which groups may not be ideal?

During this phase, include people with:
  • No or limited hearing
  • Limited vision (magnifier only)
  • Difficult with learning and memory
  • Difficulty reading (no specialised tech)
  • Neurodiverse (no specialised tech or settings)
During this phase, avoid people with:
  • May need to change the interface
  • Use a keyboard or keyboard alternative
  • Use voice control technology
  • Use screen reader and other text-to-speech
Developed products?

When testing developed products (e.g. HTML code), which types of people with disabilities could be included?

During this phase, include people with:
  • May need to change the interface
  • Use a keyboard or keyboard alternative
  • Use voice control technology
  • Use screen reader and other text-to-speech
  • Use literacy support tools

4. Recruiting

Where are you recruiting?

Within the organisation? Via loyal customers? Through a recruitment agency? Other?

Things to consider:
  • Don’t over-burden people within the organisation
  • Engaging with loyal customers shows that you care
Special considerations?

What special accessibility considerations may be necessary if considering a recruitment agency?

Things to consider:
  • Does the agency have experience with people with disabilities
  • Does the agency have access to a broad disability audience
  • Does the agency have a barrier-free recruitment process
Recruitment information?

If you want to include people with disabilities, what specific information should you consider as part of the recruitment specifications?

Things to consider:
  • The user need, or the disability
  • The number of people with each disability
  • Any specific assistive technologies
  • Technical proficiency, particularly with assistive technologies
How many people?

How many people with disabilities should you include in the research process?
