The end goal of inclusive design

Inclusive design is about designing products or services to be usable for as many people as possible.

Inclusive design is particularly important for groups who are often marginalised and excluded.

In its simplest form, inclusive design means involving a wide range of people in key processes such as:

  • Surveys
  • Interviews
  • User testing sessions
  • Design reviews

Ideally, we want co-creation!

This is where input from key audiences plays a central role from beginning to end - during planning, design and development processes.

The importance of observing people in action

One of the most important things you can do if designing or building a product is to spend time observing people.

In the digital world, this means observing people interact with web content, web apps, native apps.

And, you should engage with a diverse range of people, including those with disabilities.

But what does this engagement actually mean?

Discussions and interviews
  • Talk to diverse groups of people.
  • Find out how they interact with the digital world.
  • Find out their needs and frustrations.
User testing
  • Observe them in their own enviroments, using their own setups.
  • Observe them doing "real world" tasks.

It is perfectly acceptable to combine different methods into one session, but user-testing should be formalised.

Some realisations:
  1. Everyone is unique.
  2. Everyone approaches tasks in their own way.
  3. It is dangerous to assume a group of people will all act in the same way.

I was luck enough to work with a mentor who totally believed in this approach.

So, from early 2003 onwards, we met with may people with a wide range of disabilities.

We went to their houses or offices, we observed them in action and we asked them questions about their experience.

For this presentation, I’m going to share some different stories from over the years.

Story 1: Who buys car insurance?

Unconscious bias could affect any or all of your research processes.

You can take a range of tests to determine any of your unconscious biases.

If you were a design researcher, who would you interview for a new car insurance product?

  • People without disabilities?
  • People who are blind?
  • People who use wheelchairs?

People who are blind may make key financial decisions in their families.

Many people with mobility issues drive modified vehicles.

Video 1 | Video 2

During customer interviews at an insuarance commpany, one researcher found an entirely new audience.

People with limited movement often need to modify their vehicles, which is very expensive.

Insurance companies often overlook people with modifield vehicles.

A lot of these people where frustrated.

When asked about products that could help them, the researcher realised that these people were highly motivated to buy insurance.

And they had an active community.

The researcher began exploring new car insurance options for this audience.

This would never have happened if they had approached the research process with biases.

Story 2: Judith and the touch screen

Judith has Cerebral Palsy and uses a Head wand.

Problem

Judith was invited to try a newly designed banking touchpad system.

Unfortunately, the designers had never considered that people use all sorts of pointer devices.

Judith’s head wand was not able to register on the touch display.

The tester actually told her:

‘You will have to tap the screen much harder!’

Result

Judith was not able to interact with the banking interface.

Take aways:
  • Invite a wide range of different types of people into the design process as early as possible.

Story 3: Judith and visible focus

Problem

Judith relies on keyboard navigation, and therefore needs to see which element is currently in focus.

Unfortunately, the site was designed without an identifiable focus states for interactive elements.

Result

Judith was not able to identify when she was focussed on the ‘Buy’ button and could not purchase any products.

Take aways:
  • Make focus states clearly visible.

Story 4: Judith and endless scrolling

Problem

Judith navigates through websites via the TAB key.

Unfortunately, the site was designed so that users could never bypass the endless scroll.

Result

Judith was not able to access footer links, such as the site contact details.

Take aways:
  • Allow users to bypass key sections of the site e.g. navigation links, endless scrolls.

Story 5: Mary and the flyout menu

Mary has low vision and uses ZoomText (Screen magnifier software).

Problem

Mary did not notice a flayout menu that would slide in from the right side of the screen because her screen magnitication did not show this portion of the screen.

Result

Mary was not able to access her banking details and so could not complete the task.

Take aways
  • Focus management is critical for all users.
  • Be aware that proximity is important and users may only see a portion of the screen.

Story 6: Dan and face tracking

Dan has a spinal injury and cannot use his hands to navigate.

He relies on Mac facial recognition to move the mouse, and facial gestures to control selecting and clicking objects

During a user-testing session, Damien expressed frustration at having to type information rather than use a dropdown or have autocomplete available, as typing is much harder for him.

Take aways:
  • Be aware that some users cannot easily type.
  • Autocomplete, dropdowns and date pickers are good for some users.
