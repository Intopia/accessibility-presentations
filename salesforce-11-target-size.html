Target size

Targets are the parts of the screen that respond to user input.

This might be with a mouse, head-wand, stylus, or by touch.

Creating targets with accessibility in mind will make it easier for users to complete tasks and navigate through your product.

Larger target size benefits a range of people with disabilities. This might include:

1. People with difficulty with fine motor control (who may find larger targets easier to select).

2. People with low vision (when used along with larger visible targets).

But everyone benefits from large targets, whether they use a touch device or a mouse.

This could include someone using a device one handed, or using it while walking where it is harder to keep the device steady.

WCAG 2.2 AA recommends a minimum target size of 24 x 24 px, but this is significantly smaller than standard industry recommendations.

Touch target sizes feature in Google’s Material Design guidelines and Apple's Human Interface Guidelines.

These specs recommend that touch targets be at least:

  • 48 x 48 px (Google)
  • 44 x 44 px (Apple)

This is in line with WCAG 2.2 AAA, which recommends meeting 44 x 44 px.

Padding

Touch targets can extend beyond the visual bounds of a component.

For example, a button may visually appear to be 32 x 32 CSS pixels but with padding, the touch target is 44 x 44 pixels.

It is also good practice to have a small amount of space between targets to prevent overlap.

The BBC Mobile Accessibility Guidelines recommends a minimum of 1 pixel, although a bit more is preferable.

Target design

Target visual design should consider colour and visibility.

Targets are part of a website’s UI, which means they should meet a 3:1 minimum colour contrast ratio.

Targets should be positioned in close proximity to any relevant labels or information to improve their usability for people with low vision or cognitive differences.

For example, on an accordion, a chevron icon helps to indicate that the accordion is interactive.

This is the target a user will click to expand the accordion.

What if that icon sits away from the text that identifies the accordion?

Anyone who can’t view the text and the icon in the same field of vision is not going to get that information together.

Ideally, we want the target closer to the label text.

Have a look at this Pause button on the Trailhead site.

These are a large 48 by 48 pixels, which makes them highly visible and easier to use.

Other buttons on the page have similarly large sizes.

Comparatively, this pause button on the Dreamforce page is around 30 by 30 pixels.

It meets the minimum requirements, but not the recommended size for ease of use.

Conclusion

Larger target sizes, with clear and visible target design, will assist users to more efficiently and easily complete tasks.
