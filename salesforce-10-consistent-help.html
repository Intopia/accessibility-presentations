Consistent Help, Redundant Entry, Accessible Authentication

In 2023, WCAG 2.2 introduced a handful of new success criteria.

There are three criteria that fall under the principle of making products “Understandable”:

  • Consistent Help
  • Redundant Entry
  • Accessible Authentication (Minimum and Enhanced versions)

Let's explore these three criteria to see how they intend to make interacting with our digital platforms easier for more people.

Consistent Help

When users are completing a task on our site or app, we need to ensure that they can find help consistently when they need it.

This is particularly important for people with cognitive variations or vision impairments.

If a person can't get the help they need, they might make a mistake or abandon what they're trying to do.

They may also be forced to seek assistance from other people.

This may involve sharing personal information, which could be a security or privacy risk.

Success Criterion 3.2.6 Consistent Help mandates that any help mechanism must be in a consistent location across your product.

This includes the visual location as well as the location for assistive technologies like screen readers.

This applies to any mechanism of help.

Whether it’s finding a phone number or email address, accessing a contact form, reading an FAQ page, or activating a chatbot.

For example, a website offers help links to a contact form and social media channels.

These links are in the footer, which means they're consistently located across the site.

By providing this consistency, we make it easier for customers to locate the help they need in a timely manner.

On the Salesforce website, the ‘Contact Us’ feature remains in the top right corner.

The ‘Support’ menu is accessed from the global nav, which is always accessible.

There is also Agentforce that appears at the bottom on page load.

Redundant Entry

What is Success Criterion 3.3.7 Redundant Entry about?

Making sure a person doesn't have to manually enter the same information more than once while they're completing a process.

This will reduce both the mental and physical effort for users.

It’s particularly important for people with limited cognitive or physical energy, or with limited memory, focus, or dexterity.

A common example of repeated information is when a person is asked for a billing and shipping address at checkout.

Often, a person's billing and shipping address are the same.

There are a few methods we can use to prevent redundant entry and reduce the effort required:

1. Allow copy-paste on the form fields.

2. Allow people to fill in information from a drop-down list.

3. Enable autofill to complete the information.

4. Provide a ‘Copy information in x field’ mechanism (such as a ‘Billing address is same as shipping address’ checkbox).

We do not need to prevent redundant entry in the following scenarios:

1. Information related to security (such as a password).

2. Information that has expired and needs re-entry (such as a credit card).

3. And information that must be re-entered for the purpose of the exercise (such as a game).

Accessible Authentication

Authentication asks people to prove their identity in multiple ways to improve security.

Success Criterion 3.3.8 Accessible Authentication (Minimum) will ensure login methods are accessible and easy to use, as well as secure.

On salesforce.com today, the TBID is used for authentication.

This stands for "Trusted Broker ID" authentication.

Generally, this should mean that we do not have to create our own authentication solution.

If you do, please carefully review the following guidance.

An accessible login method doesn't require people to remember, transcribe, or solve information without help.

These are considered cognitive function tests, and they can make logging in very difficult for some people.

We’ll go through each example now.

Remember

People with cognitive variations may have trouble remembering non-personal information, including usernames and passwords.

We should enable mechanisms to assist people with recall.

This might include:

  • Allowing them to copy-paste in a password.
  • Or allowing autofill on the form.
Transcribe

People with cognitive and learning disorders, vision impairments or physical impairments may struggle to accurately and easily transcribe information.

Transcription is a common feature of two factor authentication.

For example, when a user is given a code that they must manually input into a verification text field, or a word-based CAPTCHA to transcribe.

We should avoid transcription where possible.

If we must use it, we should allow users to enter the data in a way that does not involve transcription, such as:

  • Selecting it from a series of options, or
  • Copy and pasting the required information (including across devices).
Solve

Puzzles are often used in CAPTCHA-based authentication.

These methods require a person to solve a math equation, drag a shape into place, or match two items.

However, many kinds of people may have trouble solving puzzles, due to their cognition, vision, or dexterity.

Because of this, most kinds of puzzles are not permitted under this criterion.

There are two kinds of puzzle that are acceptable under the “Minimum” (AA) version of this criterion:

1. Object recognition, such as selecting all cars in a set of images.

2. Personal content recognition, where a person can upload their own non-text content, like a photo, and then identify it from a set of options.

If necessary, Salesforce can use Google reCAPTCHA, which is an acceptable ‘object recognition’ puzzle.

Keep in mind this type of CAPTCHA isn't going to work for people with no or limited vision.

So, an alternative that meets this criterion will still be necessary.

Authentication alternatives

Now that we've broken down our main requirements, we can also consider providing an alternative login method that is not a cognitive function test.

Some examples include:

1. Hardware authentication devices, such as a YubiKey.

2. Authentication with your device, such as a fingerprint or facial scan.

3. Allowing personal information like an email as an alternative to a username.

4. Responding to a security notification on a second device.

Salesforce uses anti-bot features to reduce the need to implement any CAPTCHA or authentication puzzles at all.

You can find out more about this by contacting the Digital Marketing Technology team.

Conclusion

These new WCAG 2.2 criteria are intended to reduce the barriers between our users and completing a task online.

By working with them, we can assist users to better understand and interact with digital products.
