Common accessibility mistakes and solutions

It can feel overwhelming to think about all the possible accessibility issues that might happen in marketing.

One way to make the process simpler is to focus on one group of people at a time, and consider their specific needs.

In this section, we’ll look at six groups:

  • People with no vision
  • People with reduced vision
  • People with reduced colour vision
  • People with reduced movement
  • People with varied learning or cognition
  • People with reduced or no hearing

1. People with no vision

  • Mistake: Word and PDF files that are unstructured.
  • Solution: Use styles in Word, which helps create accessible PDFs with tags.
Andrew (person who is blind)

“If I come across a PDF without tags, I know it is going to be painful.”

  • Mistake: Image-only content without text alternatives.
  • Solution: Provide meaningful alt text and ensure text is available outside images.
Neil (person who is blind)

“Images are important for me to understand the purpose and emotion of the page.”

2. People with reduced vision

  • Mistake: Layouts that don’t adjust to peoples neeeds.
  • Solution: Allow layouts to rescale and adapt.
Katy

We will talk about content that breaks soon!

  • Mistake: Right-aligned placement of critical elements.
  • Solution: Keep layouts consistent, with predictable positioning of buttons and calls-to-action.
Mary (person with reduced vision)

“That button was so far over to the right that I almost missed it completely.”

People with reduced colour vision

  • Mistake: Text and backgrounds with insufficient contrast.
  • Solution: Solution: Choose accessible colour palettes that meet WCAG contrast ratios.
Mary (person with reduced vision)

“That text is a bit hard to see. I’m going to paste it into Word so I can read it.”

  • Mistake: Relying on colour alone to convey meaning.
  • Solution: Solution: Add icons, labels, or text alternatives.
Barry (Protanopia)

“That graph is impossible to read. I can’t see the difference in the colours”

People with reduced movement

  • Mistake: Interactive content that is not keyboard accessible.
  • Solution: Ensure all interactive elements are keyboard accessible and have clear focus states.
Judith (Cerebral Palsy)

“I can’t use that dropdown.”

We will talk about other issues with Dan soon!

People with varied learning or cognition

  • Mistake: Overly dense layouts or cluttered design.
  • Solution: Keep layouts simple, with clear headings and chunked information.
Russ (Dyslexia)

I need headings to quickly scan content.

  • Mistake: Distracting animations, autoplaying audio, or complex navigation.
  • Solution: Give users control over media and provide simple, predictable paths.
Tess

“That animation is giving me a migraine.”

People with reduced or no hearing

  • Mistake: Videos and audios without captions.
  • Solution: Provide human-checked captions for all spoken content.
  • Mistake: Videos and audios without transcripts.
  • Solution: Where possible, provide transcripts alongside video and audio.

Managing accessibility within real-world constraints

Some quick tips to help teams deliver accessible marketing within typical budget and time pressures.

Principles

1. Prioritise high-impact fixes:

Focus first on fixes that benefit the largest number of people.

2. Aim for progress, not perfection:

Even partial improvements reduce barriers and move you forward.

3. Design with inclusion in mind:

Build content and campaigns that adapt to different needs, so accessibility doesn’t feel like an afterthought.

4. Build team knowledge:

Educate designers, marketers, and content creators so accessibility becomes part of work.

Practice

1. Create templates, patterns and checklists:

Develop reusable templates, brand guidelines, and checklists to avoid repeating the same mistakes.

2. Leverage accessibility tools:

Use accessibility tools to help quickly identify issues.

3. Integrate accessibility into sign-off:

Make accessibility checks (contrast, captions, alt text) part of the approval process.

Simple accessibility tools you could use today

Here are some tools you can use to improve accessibility.

Colour contrast checkers:

Tools like WebAIM Contrast Checker or browser extensions to test text/background contrast.

Reading level checkers:

Built-in readability stats in Word, Hemingway Editor, or online tools to keep content simple and clear.

Word accessibility checker:

Microsoft Word has an Accessibility Checker that flags issues like missing alt text or poor structure.

PowerPoint accessibility checker:

Useful for campaign decks and presentations, checks for missing slide titles, reading order, etc.

PDF accessibility tools:

Adobe Acrobat Pro’s Accessibility Check for tagging and reading order issues.

Alt text helpers:

Built-in suggestions in Word, PowerPoint, and LinkedIn (with manual edits for accuracy).

Captioning tools:

Auto-captioning in YouTube or Vimeo - with the option to edit for accuracy.
