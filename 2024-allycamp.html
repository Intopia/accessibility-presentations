What is WCAG?

The World Wide Web Consortium (W3C) develops international standards for the World Wide Web.

One of these standards is the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines - often called ‘WCAG’.

These guidelines explain how to make web content more accessible to people with disabilities.

The current standard is WCAG 2.2, launched on 5 October 2023.

Success Criteria

WCAG 2.2 contains 87 unique Success Criteria.

These are a series of testable criteria that help determine if your digital content can be considered ‘accessible’.

Each of these Success Criteria has a compliance level of: ‘A’, ‘AA’, or ‘AAA’.

In order to be ‘AA’ compliant, sites must meet all ‘A’ and ‘AA’ Success Criteria - 56 criteria in total.

Level A

Your team MUST satisfy these checkpoints. Otherwise, one or more groups will find it impossible to access information in the document.

Level AA

Your team SHOULD satisfy these checkpoints. Otherwise, one or more groups will find it difficult to access information.

Level AAA

Your team MAY address these checkpoints, Otherwise, one or more groups will find it somewhat difficult to access information.

Why should you care about accessibility?

Key reasons to care:
  1. Legal responsibilities
  2. Reputation
  3. Improved user experience
  4. Commercial incentives

1. Legal responsibilities

All public-facing digital products in Australia are required to comply with the Disability Discrimination Act 1992.

Australian public-facing digital products must also conform to WCAG 2.0 AA via the Australian Human Rights Commission Advisory Notes.

2. Reputation

Some examples of legal cases
  • 2019, USA: Guillermo Robles v. Domino’s Pizza
  • 2015, Australia: Gisele Mesnage v. Coles
  • 2000, Australia: Bruce Maguire v. SOCOG

While there are financial risks associated with legal actions, the possible damage to an organisation’s reputation is far more significant.

Domino’s will likely forever be remembered as the brand that argued against the basic rights of a blind man - and lost.

Source

3. Improved user experience

Features intended for people with disabilities often improve the user experience for many other groups.

Ramps designed for wheelchair users also aid parents with prams, people with trollies and anyone with mobility issues.

SMS texting was invented by Finnish inventors to help deaf people to communicate without speaking.

Captions designed for people with hearing loss also help everyone better understand and concentrate on video content.

4. Commercial incentives

Do you know the percentage of people who have some form of disability in Australia?

Some stats
  • Australia: 21.4% of the population
  • New Zealand: 24.3% of the population
  • USA: 26% of the population

These estimates are known to be low, as people:

  • May not identify themselves as having a disability.
  • May not want to disclose information on their disability.
  • May not be aware that they have a disability.
Bottom line?

Can you afford to alienate potential or existing customers due to inaccessible products and services?

Inclusive language

Inclusive language is an integral part of recognising and respecting diversity.

Identity-first language (IFL)

Based on the idea that a person’s disability is part of their identity.

Examples
  • ‘Disabled person’
  • ‘Blind person’
  • ‘Wheelchair user’
People-first language (PFL)

Based on the idea that a person is not defined by their disability.

Examples
  • ‘Person with a disability’
  • ‘Person who is blind’
  • ‘Person who uses a wheelchair’
Some community-driven exceptions
  • ‘Deaf person’
  • ‘Autistic person’
Always ask people their preferences
  • People may have their own preferences regarding identity.
  • Some people may not identify as having a disability at all.

Avoid ableist language

Avoid using language that frames people with disabilities as people to be pitied or ridiculed.

Abelist words
Instead of: Try this:
Stupid, retarded, dumb, etc Ignorant, uninformed
Crazy, insane, nuts, psycho, etc. Wild, bizarre, outrageous
Lame Boring, unexciting
Abelist idioms
Instead of: Try this:
‘Blind leading the blind’ Uninformed leading the uninformed?
‘Blindspot’ Something missed?
‘Falling on deaf ears’ Not listening or absorbing?
‘Tone-deaf’ Inconsiderate
Negative phrases
Instead of: Try this:
Suffers from... Lives with...
Confined to a wheelchair Uses a wheelchair
Wheelchair-bound Uses a wheelchair

Avoid sensationalisation

Avoid treating regular people with disabilities as ‘brave’ or ‘inspirational’ for simply existing and going about their daily lives.

Examples of sensationalisation
  • ‘He is so brave’
  • ‘She is an inspiration’

Only mention when relevant

A person’s disability should only be mentioned if relevant to the context.

Is the term ‘disability’ ok?

Some people prefer to be referred to as ‘differently-abled’. Others prefer the term ‘disabled’.

As before, ask the individual for their preferences where possible.

Avoid terms like ‘normal’

Don’t use the word ‘normal’ to describe a person without a disability.

It’s normal for some people to have disabilities, whether permanent or temporary, congenital or acquired.

  • ‘Non-sighted people’ and ‘Sighted people’
  • ‘Deaf people’ and ‘Hearing people’
  • ‘Neurodiverse’ and ‘Neurotypical’
  • ‘Person with a disability’ and ‘Person without a disability’

Let’s meet some people

We’re going to look a range of different people and how they interact with the digital world.

  1. People with no vision
  2. People with reduced vision
  3. People with reduced colour vision
  4. People with reduced movement
  5. People with varied learning or cognition
  6. People with reduced or no hearing

1. People with no vision

May use:
  • A keyboard - without a mouse
  • Screen reader software (e.g. JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, Narrator)
  • Refreshable braille devices (e.g. Stand-alone, Smart displays)

2. People with reduced vision

May use:
  • System settings (e.g. magnification, font size, spacing, colour)
  • Browser-based page zoom (e.g. increase to 400%)
  • Increase text-size only (e.g. increase text to 200%)
  • Screen magnifier software (e.g. ZoomText)

3. People with reduced colour vision

May need:
  • Good colour contrast
  • Readable fonts
  • The ability to magnify or zoom content
  • The ability to customise the UI

4. People with reduced movement

May use:
  • Head pointer
  • Sip and puff
  • Head switch
  • Voice recognition software (e.g. Dragon NaturallySpeaking)

5. People with varied learning or cognition

May use:
  • ‘Text-to-speech’ and ‘Speech-to-text’ software
  • Screen masking software (e.g. Read&Write)
  • Spelling and grammar software (e.g. Grammarly, MS Word)
  • Screen reader software (e.g. JAWS, NVDA, VoiceOver, Narrator)

6. People with reduced or no hearing

May need:
  • Transcript - text version in separate document
  • Captions - synchronised text presented within media
  • Sign language translations

Disabilities are varied and complex

Disabilities may be spectrums - such as ADHD, ASD, Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorder.

Some people have multiple disabilities or conditions.

Some disabilities change over time such as:

  • Deteriorating eye sight or hearing.
  • Improved functionality after a stroke.
  • Changes due to different treatment or medication.
  • Changes from day to day.

Any of these people could be your customers today or tomorrow!

Accessibility user profiles

How can we plan, design and build for all the different types of disabilities?

One solution is to create a set of user profiles that follow the broad categories we just reviewed.

  1. A person with no vision
  2. A person with low vision
  3. A person with reduced colour vision
  4. A person with limited movement
  5. A person with varied learning or cognition
  6. A person with reduced or no hearing
Claudia
Key info:

Claudia is a 54-year-old social worker with low vision.

Devices and technology:

Claudia uses ZoomText, a large monitor and a high-visibility keyboard.

She changes default colours to increase contrast.

She uses the ‘speech to text’ feature on her smartphone.

Frustrations:

Claudia tends to forget to scroll horizontally when using ZoomText.

She sometimes misses form fields or help text when they’re next to each other rather than stacked.

She hates it when pop-up boxes appear off-screen, as she needs to scroll to find and close the pop-up.

They allow teams to focus on:
  • People rather than compliance.
  • Different types of people and their individual needs.
  • One profile at a time.

When should these profiles be used?

These profiles have the greatest impact when used during the early stages of design, development and testing processes.

  • A user-journey (e.g. Getting an insurance quote)
  • A process (e.g. Making a payment)
  • A page (e.g. Filling in a form)
  • A component (e.g. Choosing a date from a date picker)

As a starting point, you could review the Gov.UK accessibility user profiles.
