Accessible motion in design

When used well, animation can improve the user experience.

It helps guide attention, provides feedback on user actions, and shows that the interface is responding.

It supports context changes — like loading new content.

It also helps users build a mental model of where things go, even when they move off screen.

For example, on this Salesforce page, the tile enlarges slightly when the mouse hovers over it.

This is a helpful indicator that a component is interactive.

Example: https://www.salesforce.com/plus/

Notes: Small example of helpful motion is the slight enlargement of a tile when mouse hovers over it, as on home page.

But motion isn’t always helpful.

For some people, motion can cause discomfort, disorientation, dizziness, nausea or headaches.

In some cases, it could trigger serious health issues like migraines or seizures.

For many people, motion can be distracting, especially when they’re trying to read or focus on a task.

Who Is Affected

Motion can affect more users than you might expect.

People with vestibular disorders, migraines, or epilepsy may react physically to certain effects.

Those with ADHD or autism can be easily distracted by movement.

And people who read more slowly may struggle if content moves away too quickly.

Things to Avoid

Avoid long animations. Keep it under five seconds.

Long animations can delay interaction, frustrate users, or contribute to motion sickness.

Avoid large or sweeping animations, especially when objects move across a lot of screen space.

This kind of motion can be disorienting, especially for people with balance or sensory sensitivities.

Be cautious with splash animations or background videos, particularly when they autoplay or cover the entire screen.

These can feel overwhelming and hard to ignore.

Additionally, background animation must not create visibility issues for viewers.

Any content laid over a background animation must be tested for colour contrast against the background at its lowest contrast point.

For example, on this Salesforce page, moving video appears as a background behind splash text.

This may be difficult for people who are sensitive to motion.

However, the video has a pause button which allows a user to pause the movement.

Example: https://www.salesforce.com/dreamforce/

Notes: Example is of video behind text. Use the moving logo grid on salesforce.com.

Avoid effects like parallax scrolling or looping animations that keep moving without stopping.

Constant motion can be tiring or distracting for many users.

Watch out for motion that goes in a different direction than the user is scrolling — like horizontal sliding while scrolling down the page.

That mismatch can feel jarring and confusing.

And finally, avoid anything that flashes quickly, starts moving automatically, or doesn’t give users a way to pause or stop it.

These kinds of effects can be inaccessible and even unsafe for some people.

Making Motion Accessible

Only use motion when it adds real value.

Animation should serve a purpose, such as showing feedback, reinforcing a state change, or guiding attention.

Avoid adding motion purely for decoration, as it can create distractions without helping the user.

For example, on this Salesforce page, there is a looping piece of video at the top of the page.

There is no way to pause or remove this animation.

Example of looping part of video: https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/video/gen-z-ai-agent-financial-perspectives/

Keep animations short.

Keep it under five seconds so as not to delay interaction, frustrate users, or contribute to motion sickness.

Make movements small and subtle.

Large, sweeping gestures are more likely to be disorienting.

Instead, use gentle fades, short transitions, or minimal shifts in position to create the effect you need.

For example, on this Salesforce page, large spinning tiles may disorient or discomfort motion-sensitive viewers.

Problematic example: https://www.salesforce.com/dreamforce/

Notes: See the way the tiles spin under ‘Big names, bigger ideas’ section.

Never rely on motion to complete a task.

If important content is only visible through animation, or an action only works by dragging or swiping with animation, that creates a barrier.

The experience must always work without animation.

Avoid motion that starts automatically.

Let users trigger it themselves, especially for anything more than a quick transition.

This gives people more control over their experience and reduces the chance of surprise or discomfort.

Always respect user preferences.

Many people set their operating system to reduce motion.

By using the prefers-reduced-motion media query in your styles, you can automatically tone down or remove animation for those who’ve asked for it.

But don’t assume that all users affected by motion will have the Reduced Motion setting on.

Many people affected by motion don’t know the Reduced Motion setting exists, or choose not to have it on.

It is not a green light to use motion as we please.

For motion that loads automatically, give users a visible option to pause or disable motion in your interface.

This feature, such as a pause button, should be an appropriate visual and target size, should meet colour contrast requirements, and should be keyboard accessible.

Reference: prefers-reduced-motion implementation tips from web.dev | implementation tips from MDN web docs

And finally, test your designs with motion turned off.

This helps ensure that your interface is still understandable, usable, and accessible without animation.

Conclusion

Motion can be a powerful tool in design — but only when it’s used with care.

By keeping it purposeful, gentle, and optional, we can make digital experiences more inclusive for everyone.
