Introduction to digital accessibility

Benevolent Society

Why should you care about digital accessibility?

Key reasons to care:
  1. Legal responsibilities
  2. Reputation
  3. Improved user experience
  4. Commercial incentives

1. Legal responsibilities

All public-facing digital products in Australia are required to comply with the Disability Discrimination Act 1992.

The Guidelines on Equal Access to Digital Goods and Services explain your legal obligations under the Disability Discrimination Act.

Organisations should conform with WCAG 2.2 at a minimum Level AA and consider appropriate Level AAA success criteria such as video transcripts, audio contrast, clear links and section headings.

Source

Some examples of legal cases
  • 2019, USA: Guillermo Robles v. Domino’s Pizza
  • 2015, Australia: Gisele Mesnage v. Coles
  • 2000, Australia: Bruce Maguire v. SOCOG

2. Reputation

Legal action can be costly, but the damage to an organisation’s reputation could be even worse.

No organisation wants to end up in the headlines for the wrong reasons:

Domino’s will likely forever be remembered as the brand that argued against the basic rights of a blind man - and lost.

Source

Supermarket giant Coles has agreed to improve its online shopping website so that it can be more easily accessed by blind people, after being taken to court for alleged disability discrimination.

Source

3. Improved user experience

Features intended for people with disabilities often improve the user experience for many other groups.

Dark mode / high-contrast mode
  • For: people with light sensitivity or visual impairments
  • Also helps: users in dark rooms, reducing eye strain, conserving battery
Text-to-speech and audiobooks
  • For: people with dyslexia or visual impairments
  • Also helps: commuters, multitaskers, language learners
Voice assistants / voice control
  • For: people with limited mobility
  • Also helps: people multitasking
Keyboard shortcuts
  • For: users with motor impairments
  • Also helps: power users, anyone seeking speed
Adjustable font sizes and zoom
  • For: people with low vision
  • Also helps: older people, people with tired eyes
Colour-blind-friendly design
  • For: people with color vision deficiencies
  • Also helps: everyone on low-contrast screens or in poor lighting

4. Commercial incentives

Do you know the percentage of people who have some form of disability in Australia?

Some stats
  • Australia: 21.4% of the population
  • New Zealand: 24.3% of the population
  • USA: 26% of the population

These estimates are known to be low, as people:

  • May not identify themselves as having a disability.
  • May not want to disclose information on their disability.
  • May not be aware that they have a disability.

Research shows that 80% of autistic females remain undiagnosed or misdiagnosed at the age of 18. Part of the problem is the research that defined the diagnostic criteria was conducted mainly on male samples, so it doesn't always apply to females or non-binary people.

Source

So the real number is likely higher — about 1 in 5 Australians.

Why should you care?

You could attract more visitors, improve customer experience, and position your organisation as an inclusive and caring brand.

Who could be trying to access your content?

  1. People with no vision
  2. People with reduced vision
  3. People with reduced colour vision
  4. People with reduced movement
  5. People with varied learning or cognition
  6. People with reduced or no hearing
  7. People with affected or no speech

What assistive technologies could they be using?

  • Screen readers
  • Refreshable braille devices
  • Zoom Text
  • Head or mouth switches
  • Voice control software
  • Face or eye tracking software
